The Chicago breakwater system extends from just north of Navy Pier to where you are now. Its history is rooted in protecting one of the region's greatest assetsâ€”
shipping on the Chicago River. In the mid-1800s, rock was placed near the river mouth to shield against heavy waves and prevent sand from clogging the entrance to the river. Over more than a century, these structures helped the city become one of the busiest commercial centers in the world. Over time, the breakwater system was expanded to protect the surrounding shoreline from heavy waves that cause flooding and erosion, a function it continues to serve. Today, hundreds of recreational boaters also take advantage of the calm water near downtown to dock their boats and safely move between the river and the lake.

The breakwater is not without its downsides. The structures require constant, and often costly, maintenance. Over the decades, portions of the breakwater have been entirely rebuilt after the base of the structure collapsed. Also, by their very design, breakwaters change the natural movement of water and sediment. Sand building up along the man-made structure helps keep rivers and harbors open. But this prevents sand from replenishing beaches down shore.
