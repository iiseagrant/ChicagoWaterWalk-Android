If you walk farther south, you will see a long, flat stretch of grass. Come back in about 20 years and you won't recognize the place. By then, the flat land that once covered the southern half will have been reshaped into woodlands, savannahs, and prairies, with wetlands and artificial reefs hugging the western shore. A little farther north, you will be able to attend plays and concerts at the new amphitheater, visit the Northerly Island Education Center, and take a stroll down the harbor boardwalk. Families will also find room for picnicking and camping throughout the peninsula.

The plan for the south side of the Northerly Island is to make the lakefront look more like it did before the city was settled. This will bring back habitats for millions of migratory birds and other land animals. The restoration will offer much-needed stopover spots for songbirds, ducks, hawks, and more as they fly north and south each year. Just off shore, rocks and underwater vegetation—two things that are scarce along the Chicago lakefront—will also help native fish species find food, avoid predators, and reproduce. These projects began in 2012 and are expected to take about five years—two years for construction and another three years for the new plants and habitats to take hold.
