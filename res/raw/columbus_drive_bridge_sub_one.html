At the turn of the 20th century, the flow of the river was reversed to protect Lake Michigan, the city's primary source of drinking water, by sending Chicago's waste to the Mississippi River. Despite this engineering feat, billions of gallons of untreated sewage, industrial pollution, and stormwater flow from the river into the lake every year. Less than an inch of rain is all it takes to push polluted water out of city and suburban sewers and into the river. To prevent flooding in the city during the biggest storms, officials are forced to open the locks that separate the river from Lake Michigan. When the locks are opened, the Chicago River resumes its original course, and its contaminated waters are flushed directly into the lake.Â
