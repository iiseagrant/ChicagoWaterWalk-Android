Despite what its name suggests, the Chicago River is actually a system of rivers and canals designed to open up commercial shipping between the Gulf of Mexico and the Great Lakes. In the years before railroads came to the Midwest, canals helped transform Chicago into a national shipping and transportation hub. The first was the Illinois and Michigan Canal. When it was completed in 1848, the channel ran 96 miles to connect the Chicago and Mississippi rivers for the first time. Â 

The city later built a second waterway, this time with the goal of reversing the flow of the Chicago River away from Lake Michigan. After it was finished in 1900, commercial shipping relocated to the new Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal. Its wider banks and shorter length made it easier for boats to navigate and made the Illinois and Michigan Canal unnecessary. The Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal is now the only link between the Great Lakes and the Gulf of Mexico. Its 28 miles is controlled by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers to prevent the spread of invasive species into the Great Lakes and ensure that the amount of water the canal pulls from Lake Michigan stays below federal limits.
